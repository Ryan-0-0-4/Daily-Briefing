Strait of Hormuz Shutdown Triggers History's Largest Oil Supply Disruption

Since the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz on February 28 — following US-Israeli strikes that killed Iran's supreme leader — tanker traffic has collapsed by over 90%, cutting roughly 10 million barrels per day from global supply. Brent crude reached $126/barrel at peak and sits above $110 today, its highest level in four years.

WHY IT MATTERS

The Strait of Hormuz handles roughly 20% of the world's seaborne oil trade and approximately the same share of global LNG supply. Unlike previous geopolitical threats to the strait — which never materialized into actual closures — this one is real and sustained. Alternative pipeline routes through Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Iraq have a combined capacity of only about 3 million barrels per day, nowhere near enough to compensate. Every week the blockade persists deepens inflationary pressure worldwide, strains airline and shipping economics, and risks secondary supply shocks in aluminum, fertilizer, and helium markets that all transit or derive from the Gulf corridor.

THE FULL PICTURE
Historical Background
The Strait of Hormuz has long been called the world's most critical oil chokepoint, but threats to close it — most prominently during the 2011–2012 Iran nuclear standoff — never translated into action. The February 28, 2026 closure is the first real disruption since the tanker wars of the 1980s and by far the most severe in the modern history of global energy markets. The IEA described it as "without precedent in the post-WWII era."
Key Players
Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) issued and enforced vessel-passage prohibitions with drone and mine threats. The US Fifth Fleet and coalition naval forces are attempting to protect some shipping, but tanker insurers and operators are unwilling to assume the risk. Saudi Arabia and the UAE are pumping at capacity through alternate overland pipelines, but existing infrastructure cannot absorb the lost volumes. OPEC+ convened an emergency call this week; members with blocked exports have limited room to maneuver.
What to Watch Next
Iran's dialogue with Oman over a ship-monitoring protocol is the most significant near-term signal. Even partial commercial shipping resumption could drop prices by $10–20/barrel rapidly. Conversely, any US strike on Iranian oil infrastructure — which Trump signaled as possible this week — could spike Brent toward $140+. Watch weekly EIA crude inventory draws and tanker AIS tracking data for signs of resumed Hormuz transit.

↳ The Hormuz closure is the most severe energy shock since the 1970s oil embargo. Until diplomatic resolution or a decisive military outcome, oil above $100 is structural — not a transient spike — and global inflation expectations need to be repriced accordingly.

THE FULL PICTURE
Historical Background
Trump's April 2, 2025 executive order declared a national emergency over persistent US trade deficits and invoked the International Emergency Economic Powers Act to apply a 10% baseline tariff on nearly all imports, with higher reciprocal rates for major trading partners beginning April 9. The move was the broadest unilateral US trade action since the Smoot-Hawley tariffs of 1930, prompting immediate retaliatory tariffs from China, the EU, Canada, and Mexico. The administration then negotiated over 50 modifications, exemptions, and pauses over the following year.
Key Players
Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent managed the complex exemption and negotiation process. Goldman Sachs economists provided the widely-cited 64% US-importer burden estimate. The Learning Resources toy importer case — backed by the US Chamber of Commerce — provided the vehicle for the Supreme Court challenge. The Court held that a standing trade deficit does not constitute an "unusual and extraordinary threat" under IEEPA's emergency standard, removing the legal foundation for future emergency-based tariffs.
What to Watch Next
The Trump administration is drafting targeted Section 301 and Section 232 tariffs to pursue similar protectionist goals within the narrower legal authority remaining. Congress is being lobbied for explicit tariff authorization legislation. The EU, Canada, and Asian nations still maintain retaliatory measures despite the US tariff rollback, creating an asymmetric trade landscape that will dominate G7 discussions through 2026 and complicate any Trump-Xi deal.

↳ The tariff experiment is a landmark policy failure: it raised prices, destroyed manufacturing jobs, expanded the deficit, and collapsed under judicial scrutiny — but the political appetite for protectionism is stronger than ever, ensuring a second-generation version is already in design.
