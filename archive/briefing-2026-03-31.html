Its closure has severed supply chains connecting Gulf producers to Asia, Europe, and North America, forcing tankers onto costly multi-week reroutes around the Cape of Good Hope. The Full Picture
Historical precedent

The Strait of Hormuz has been threatened but never fully closed in modern history. The closest precedent was the Tanker War of 1984–1988, when Iran and Iraq attacked Gulf shipping without halting oil flows entirely. The current closure is historically unprecedented. The conflict began February 28, 2026, with joint US-Israeli airstrikes that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei at age 86 — the first assassination of an Iranian supreme leader — along with the defense minister and senior IRGC commanders. Mojtaba Khamenei was appointed successor on March 8.

Key players and forces

Iran's IRGC Navy controls the mining and patrol capabilities enforcing the closure. The US Fifth Fleet in Bahrain is the primary counterforce. Saudi Arabia and the UAE — the world's largest Gulf exporters — are most economically exposed. The IEA has activated emergency reserve releases, but these cannot compensate for the scale of disruption. IRGC commander Alireza Tangsiri was killed in an Israeli airstrike on March 26, which Iran cited as justification for formally closing the strait to allied vessels the following day.

What to watch next

President Trump's April 6 deadline is the critical near-term inflection point. If Iran does not commit to reopening the strait, Trump has threatened extensive strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure, which would likely push Brent back above $120 and trigger an unprecedented response targeting Gulf infrastructure more broadly. Any fresh maritime strikes on Saudi or Emirati assets risk pulling the GCC into direct conflict.
